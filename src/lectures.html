What Are Requirements

What leads to a useful product?

  • Situations where the developers have understood:
    • What the product intends to accomplish
    • How it must accomplish this purpose
    • What kind of work the users want to do
    • How the product affects work and fits into the organization’s goals
  • A product’s requirements are:
    • What it does for the users
    • Constraints it must satisfy in a particular context
  • Requirements process:
    • Exploration of intended product
    • Discovering behavior and functionality
    • Describing outcomes of the above activities
  • Requirements are:
    • Independent of development process, programming language, development tools, etc.
    • Expressed as abstractions
    • Technology neutral

Requirements vs. Design

Requirement: Statement of business need without implementation bias

Design is a plan that:

  • Translates requirements into physical reality
  • Determination of:
    • Devices to be used
    • Software components
    • How construction will be done

Requirements, design, and implementation can be done in an iterative loop

Requirements Gathering and Systems Modeling

  • There is a significant overlap
  • User models help find requirements
  • Requirements are used to model functionality and data
  • Both generate artifacts that can be used to understand and specify requirements

Evolution of Requirements

  • Requirements are not invariant and may evolve
  • Users may demand more functionality
  • In an agile process, partial product may have an effect on work practices leading to new requirements
  • Evolution of requirement cannot be controlled, it has to be accepted
  • The requirements process needs to explicitly take this into account

Requirements & Agile Development

We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.

What is a requirement?

Functional Requirements:

  • Action that a product must take to be useful to the user
  • Arise from work that stakeholders do

Example: The product shall produce an amended de-icing schedule when a change to a truck status means that the previously scheduled work cannot be carried out as planned

Non-Functional Requirements:

  • Critical Qualities or properties that a product must have:
    • Performance
    • Look and feel
    • Usability
    • Security
    • Legal restriction

Example: The product shall determine friend or foe in less than 0.25 seconds

Example: The product shall be able to be used by travelers in the arrivals hall who do not speak the home language

Constraints:

  • Global issues that shape requirements

Example: The product shall be available at the beginning of the new tax year

RE Process

What does the RE process represent?

  • Normative, prescriptive approaches:
    • Staged sequence of activities and/or task objectives
    • Intermediate outputs
    • Appropriate techniques
    • Challenges and problems for the specific tasks
  • Non-normative approaches:
    • Contextual non-deterministic
    • A description of the socio-behavioral processes

Five factors influencing the RE process (Pohl 1994)

  • Methods and Methodologies
  • Tools
  • Social Aspects
  • Cognitive Skills
  • Economic Constraints

Project Blast-off

  • Ensures feasibility of project
  • Principal stakeholders meet:
    • Client
    • Main users
    • Lead requirement analyst
    • Technical and business experts
  • Ensure project has clearly defined scope and worthy objective
  • Other stakeholders are identified
  • Preliminary cost estimate / preliminary risk estimate
  • Go/No go decision

Trawling for Knowledge

  • Work context diagram is partitioned into Business Use cases
  • Different techniques:
    • Apprenticing
    • Scenarios
    • Use case workshops
  • Essence of the System vs Perceived solution

Prototyping Work

  • Prototypes:
    • Simulation of requirements
    • High fidelity vs Low fidelity
  • Effective when:
    • Requirements are not being formulated properly
    • Very novel product

Writing Requirements

  • Essential to avoid misunderstood requirements
  • How to write requirements:
    • It should be testable
    • Agreed and understood by stakeholder
    • Audience
    • Fit criterion
    • Shell/specification template
  • Integrated activity
  • Alternative – systems modeling
  • How does this help the analyst

Quality Gateway

  • Test requirements
  • Requirements tested for:
    • Completeness
    • Relevance
    • Testability
    • Coherency
    • Traceability
    • Understandability
  • Requirements leakage

Reusing Requirements

  • Products not completely unique
  • Reuse specifications from previous projects
  • Non-functional requirements are standard

Project Blastoff

  • Identifies:
    • The work area related to the product
    • The purpose of the product
  • Lays the groundwork to make the RE activity efficient and effective

Project Blastoff Deliverables

  • Purpose of the project:
    • Short quantified statement indicating the product’s intention and associated advantages
    • Reasons for investment
    • Business benefits
  • Scope of work:
    • Business Area affected
  • Stakeholders:
    • Client
    • Customer
    • Users
    • People with related knowledge/influence
  • Constraints:
    • Design solution restriction
    • Budget (Time and Money)
  • Names:
    • List of special terminology
  • Estimated cost
  • Risks
  • Low fidelity prototype

Go/No Go Decision

  • Is the project viable?
  • Are the benefits worth the cost?
  • Are there enough information to proceed with the requirements activity?

Setting the Scope

  • What is the Scope?
    • Scope of work for which the project is to be used
  • Work – Business activity for which the user needs the product
  • Involves processing activity and information
  • Scope is set by separating focal work from that surrounding it

Stakeholders

  • The customer:
    • Buys the product
    • Could sometimes be the end user
    • Useful to include information about potential customers
  • The Users: get to know them
    • Identify user categories and list their attributes
  • Possible attributes:
    • Subject Matter experience
    • Technological experience
    • Intellectual abilities
    • Attitude toward the job
    • Education
    • Linguistic Skills

Goals

  • Purpose – What should the product do?
  • Advantage – What business advantage does it provide?
  • Measurement – How do you measure such advantage?
  • Additional Attributes:
    • Viable
    • Feasible
    • Achievable
  • Supporting Material

Constraints

  • Global Requirements:
    • Affect decisions about scope of products
    • Limitations on budget
    • Limit solution space
  • Solution Constraints:
    • e.g., COTS
    • Mandated use of existing infrastructure
  • Project Constraint:
    • Budget
    • Time

Other Blastoff Deliverables

  • Naming conventions
  • Cost
  • Risk

Go/No Go

  • Are the goals clear and unambiguous?
  • Is it measurable?
  • Is benefits to organization clearly indicated?
  • Is it viable?
  • Is there agreement on work scope?
  • Do some risks have high probability of occurrence?
  • Can such risks make the project infeasible?
  • Is cost reasonable given the benefits?
  • Are there sufficient justifications to invest?
  • Are there strong reasons not to invest?
  • Are there reasons to conduct further investigations?

After Blastoff

  • Outcomes of the blastoff:
    • Scope of work
    • Work Context Diagram
  • The scope of work too large
  • Mechanisms for breaking it down

What does “Work” represent?

  • More than a computer system(s)
  • A sum total of:
    • Human task
    • Computers
    • Machines/Devices
    • Documents
    • Etc.
  • Description of relevant “business system”

Understanding the context of work

  • What information flow takes place between the work and the outside world?
  • Adjacent systems that make demand or contribute to the world:
    • Automated systems
    • People
    • Departments
    • Organization
  • Adjacent systems:
    • Customers for the service/information produced by work
    • Supply information needed by work
  • How do you identify Adjacent Systems?
    • Interfaces within organization
    • Interfaces outside your organizations
  • Work context should include all parts of existing work – human activities and computer systems

Business Events

  • Business event:
    • Takes place outside the scope of work
    • Signal to work happens through information inflow
    • Work responds to this information flow in some manner
  • Business events initiate a response through business use cases
  • Time triggered business events:
    • Occurs when a pre-arranged time is reached
    • This could be based on:
      • Periodic
      • Time Intervals
      • Time elapsing since a certain business day
  • Response characterized by outflow of information

Business Events and Business Cases

  • Allows for a partitioning of work in a non-subjective way through an identification of external stimuli
  • Helps create cohesive partitioning with minimal interfaces between the pieces (less dependencies):
    • Subsequent independent analysis can be carried out
  • Allows for a direction of investigation of what happens at the time of a business event

More on Business Use Cases

  • Pre-planned response to a Business Event
  • Collection of:
    • Identifiable processes
    • Data (stored and/or retrieved)
    • Outputs
    • Messages
  • Unit of functionality (basis for functional requirements)
  • Business cases can be isolated:
    • Stakeholders with expertise in that part of work can be identified
  • Business Cases are observable
  • Processing for business cases are continuous

Breaking Down Work

  • Characteristics of Business Use Cases that make them suitable for the partitioning:
    • Natural partitions – make obvious and natural contribution to work
    • Minimal Connectivity with other parts of the work
    • Clearly defined scope
    • Rules for defining scope
    • Observable and definable boundaries
    • Recognizable nomenclature
    • Determinable existence
    • One or more expert stakeholders available

Identifying Business Events

  • Information Flows in a context diagram
  • Each information flow is the result of a business event and/or the resultant business use case

Product Use Cases

  • Determined once the correct work of the business use case has been determined
  • It is a part of the business use case, specifically determining the role the product plays in the work’s response
  • In other words – part of the response is handled by the proposed automated system
